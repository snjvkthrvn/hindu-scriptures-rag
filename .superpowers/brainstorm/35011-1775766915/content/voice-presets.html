User-customizable voice — which presets should ship?

Same question ("What does the Gita say about dealing with fear?") rendered in five voices. Each preset maps to a different "Identity & voice" block in the system prompt — everything else (research, Sanskrit discipline, length, etc.) stays identical. Multi-select: click every voice you want in the final shipping set. Uncheck any you don't. You can also describe changes in the terminal.

WARM ELDER · DEFAULT
Fear is something the Gītā meets directly — and gently. When Krishna looks at Arjuna trembling on the battlefield, he doesn't dismiss the fear; he names its root. What Arjuna is afraid of, Krishna says, isn't death — it's losing what was never really his to keep.
BG 2.14
मात्रास्पर्शास्तु कौन्तेय
The contact of the senses with objects, he says, bring cold and heat, pleasure and pain — endure them, Arjuna. That's not stoicism; it's an invitation to loosen your grip...

1. Warm Elder ★

Friend across the kitchen table. Direct but tender. Uses "you," explains Sanskrit gently, doesn't preach. Most approachable for newcomers. This is the default.

TRADITIONAL GURU
The Bhagavad Gītā addresses the nature of fear (bhaya) with profound clarity. In the teaching of Lord Kṛṣṇa to Arjuna, we find that fear arises from two roots: attachment (rāga) to the transient, and the illusion of separateness (ahaṅkāra) from the Self.
BG 2.56
दुःखेष्वनुद्विग्नमनाः
The steady-minded one (sthitaprajña), whose mind is undisturbed in sorrow, free from longing for pleasure — it is this one, Lord Kṛṣṇa declares, whose fear has been transcended...

2. Traditional Guru

Satsang discourse. More formal, honorifics, dense Sanskrit terminology in parentheses, reverent. Closer to the current voice. For users who want the classical register.

PLAINSPOKEN FRIEND
The Gita actually has a lot to say about fear, and honestly it's pretty practical. The main insight is this: most fear comes from clinging to outcomes you can't fully control. When you let go of the clinging, the fear softens too.
BG 2.47
कर्मण्येवाधिकारस्ते
That's what "you have a right to action, not to its fruits" really means — not a command to be detached from life, but a reminder that the grip is the problem, not the action...

3. Plainspoken Friend

Zero jargon, modern language. Explains Sanskrit terms thoroughly but uses English first. Great for beginners or people burned out on "spiritual" vocabulary.

SCHOLARLY
The Bhagavad Gītā locates fear (bhaya) as a consequence of avidyā (ignorance of the Self) compounded by ahaṅkāra (ego-identification). BG 2.56 describes the sthitaprajña as duḥkheṣv anudvigna-manāḥ — "one whose mind is unshaken in sorrow" — establishing the phenomenology of fearlessness as a function of cognitive stability, not emotional suppression.
BG 2.56
दुःखेष्वनुद्विग्नमनाः
Śaṅkara reads this through the lens of jñāna-yoga: fear is eliminated when the Self is recognized as non-dual (advaita). Rāmānuja, by contrast, grounds fearlessness in devotional surrender...

4. Scholarly

Precise, dense, citation-heavy. IAST throughout, technical terminology, multiple schools weighed. For researchers and serious students.

POETIC
Fear is a thief that robs the present of its fullness. The Gītā knows this thief well. On the battlefield of Kurukshetra, Arjuna's bow slips from his hand — and Krishna does not shame him for it.
BG 2.20
न जायते म्रियते वा कदाचित्
Never born, never dying, the Self has no cause to tremble. This is not a denial of feeling — it is a reminder that the one who fears, and the one who witnesses the fear, are not the same...

5. Poetic

Lyrical, metaphor-rich, meditative. Short sentences, imagery, quote-heavy. For contemplative reading, less good for reference lookups.

Plus three mechanical decisions

H

Voice control lives in the header

Small pill between theme toggle and "New Chat" — shows current voice, click for dropdown. Always visible, one tap away.

D

Voice control lives in the history drawer

Under "+ NEW" button. Less prominent, implies voice is a per-thread setting rather than a global one.

B

Both — header for global, drawer for per-thread override

Most power, most complexity. Probably overkill for v1.

G

Voice is global — one setting for all threads

Simpler model, stored in localStorage as hsr.voice. Switching voice applies to all future answers across all threads.

T

Voice is per-thread — each conversation remembers its own

Stored with the thread in localStorage. Useful if you want a "scholarly thread" and a "warm elder thread" simultaneously. More complex.

P

Presets only (no free-form)

User picks from the voices above. Simpler, safer, no way to break the prompt.

A

Presets + an "Advanced / Custom" free-form text box

Power users can write their own voice instruction (max ~200 chars), which gets appended to the system prompt. Risk: people can break the behavior.

My recommendation

Ship all 5 voices with Warm Elder as the default. Header placement (H) — visible, discoverable, one tap. Global (G) — simplest mental model for v1; per-thread can come later if users ask for it. Presets only (P) — free-form is v2 work; the 5 presets already cover the full range of reasonable voices.
